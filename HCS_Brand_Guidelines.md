## 1. Brand Overview

Hemphill Counseling Services is a solo telehealth practice led by Carlton, a licensed clinical professional counselor (LCPC, LMHC) with over seven years of experience. The practice specializes in ADHD, OCD, and Autism in adults, including those diagnosed later in life, and provides affirming, evidence-based care for LGBTQ+ and BIPOC clients.

Carlton offers individual therapy, OCD assessment, clinical supervision, and consultation for professionals working with neurodivergent populations. Services are delivered entirely via telehealth across Arizona, California, Florida, Idaho, Illinois, and Washington.

What distinguishes this practice is not a list of modalities. It is the combination of diagnostic specificity, identity affirmation, and clinical depth, delivered by a clinician who brings both expertise and an understood sameness to the work. Clients do not have to explain themselves. Referring clinicians do not have to wonder if their client will feel seen.

**What Makes This Practice Distinct**

- **Specialization:** ADHD, OCD, and Autism, not as a general interest but as a primary clinical focus
- **Method:** Insight-first therapy using I-CBT, ERP, and CBT, applied where they genuinely help, not as default protocol
- **Identity:** LGBTQ+ and BIPOC affirming care, with particular focus on men in those communities
- **Collaboration:** Works alongside prescribers and supports clients navigating medication decisions
- **Assessment:** OCD assessment available in addition to individual therapy
- **Community:** Supervision and consultation available for clinicians working with neurodivergent populations

## 3. Tagline

**Primary tagline:**
Insight-driven therapy from a clinician who specializes in you.

**Subhead (use with tagline wherever possible):**
Neurodivergent adults (ADHD, OCD, Autism) | LGBTQ+ & BIPOC affirming | Teletherapy in AZ · CA · FL · ID · IL · WA

**How the Tagline Works**

The tagline makes a promise. The subhead fulfills it. They are designed to work together. The tagline earns attention; the subhead provides proof.

"From a clinician who specializes in you" does two things simultaneously: it signals clinical precision for referring clinicians scanning for diagnostic specificity, and it creates a recognition moment for potential clients who have been through wrong-fit therapy. It does not position other clinicians as villains. It positions Carlton as the right fit.

**Usage Notes**

- Use tagline and subhead together wherever possible: homepage hero, directory listings, email signatures, social bios
- The subhead pipe format is intentional and native to the brand. Do not replace with commas or bullet points
- The subhead is inclusive at the brand level. Men-specific positioning is deployed contextually in individual posts, the about page, and LinkedIn, not in the subhead
- Do not alter the tagline for platform fit. If space is limited, use the tagline alone, never the subhead alone
- Do not add exclamation points, questions, or urgency language around the tagline

## 4. Voice and Tone

**Tone Name:** Calm authority — the clinician who already knows you'll be okay.

Not warm-fuzzy. Not cold-clinical. This brand writes like someone who has seen this before, is not alarmed by it, and wants the reader to feel that steadiness. It educates without condescending. It validates without catastrophizing. It trusts the reader to handle the truth.

**Voice Dimensions**

| Dimension | Level |
|---|---|
| Clinical precision | Very high |
| Warmth / empathy | High |
| Authority | Very high |
| Conversational tone | Low to medium |
| Sentence length | Very short |
| Urgency / alarm | None |

**Signature Patterns**

**1. Stacked Declarative Sentences**
Each sentence lands before the next begins. No filler between them. No transitions that soften the point.
> "A thought is not a desire. It is not a plan. It is not a window into your character. It is just a thought."

**2. Staccato Adjective Triples as Resolution**
After a longer explanation, end with three punchy words or short phrases. No verb. No softening. Just the landing.
> "Compassionate. Systematic. At your pace."

**3. The Closing Signature**
Deadpan. Confident. Removes a barrier the reader didn't know they were worried about.
> "No phone calls necessary."

**Language Rules**

Use:
- Short declarative sentences
- Define clinical terms before using them
- Reframe, then redirect
- Evidence-backed claims ("Research confirms...")
- Staccato endings
- Active voice

Avoid:
- Exclamation points
- Urgency or scarcity language
- Overly warm filler phrases ("I'm so glad you're here")
- Jargon without definition
- Questions as hooks
- Em dashes (anywhere, in any content)

## 5. Messaging by Audience

The same practice. Three different conversations. Tone stays consistent. What gets emphasized shifts.

**A. Potential Clients**

This audience is often arriving after a string of wrong-fit experiences. They are not looking for warmth. They are looking for someone who will not make them explain themselves from scratch. Lead with recognition, not reassurance.

What to emphasize:
- The specific experience they're navigating: overthinking, stuck cycles, late diagnosis, identity questions
- That the approach follows how they actually think, not a neurotypical template
- That LGBTQ+ and BIPOC identity is not the problem to fix; it is the starting point
- Practical access: fully online, no phone call required

What to avoid:
- Promising transformation or outcomes
- Using urgency ("Don't wait", "Limited spots")
- Centering Carlton's credentials before the client's experience

Sample language:
> You've probably explained your brain to a lot of people. You're not going to have to do that here.
>
> Overthinking is not the problem. It is usually a signal. The work is figuring out what it's pointing to.

**B. Referring Clinicians**

This audience is scanning fast. They need three things immediately: diagnostic match, licensure coverage, and confidence that Carlton will make them look good for the referral. They are not looking for warmth. They are looking for precision.

The one sentence a referring clinician should walk away with:
> He specializes in exactly what my client needs, he's licensed in their state, he works collaboratively with prescribers, and he gets it without having to say so.

What to emphasize:
- Licensure: LCPC, LMHC, BC-TMH across AZ, CA, FL, ID, IL, WA
- Diagnostic specificity: ADHD, OCD, Autism, including late-diagnosed adults
- Methods: ERP, I-CBT, CBT, applied where they genuinely help
- Collaboration with prescribers
- Population fit: LGBTQ+ and BIPOC adults, with particular focus on men
- Understood sameness: do not over-explain; let it be legible
- Consultation and supervision available

What to avoid:
- Generic affirming language without clinical specificity
- Warmth-first copy
- Burying the licensure and state coverage

**A note on "lived experience" language:** Carlton does not lead with personal disclosure in his clinical voice. The positioning is understood sameness. A referring clinician who knows Carlton's background can tell a client "he gets it" and the client does not need it explained. Do not perform this. Do not over-narrate it. Let the positioning speak.

**C. Supervisees and Consultation Clients**

What to emphasize:
- Carlton's specialization in neurodiversity-affirming and culturally responsive telehealth
- The availability of both supervision (licensed and unlicensed) and consultation
- Practical, targeted guidance, not abstract theory
- Experience working with ADHD, OCD, Autism, LGBTQ+, and BIPOC populations

What to avoid:
- Positioning this service as mentorship or coaching. It is clinical.
- Softening the expertise signal

## 6. Platform-Specific Guidance

**A. Instagram (Primary Social)**

Post structure:
- Line 1: A declarative statement that does not need context to land
- Lines 2-4: Short stacked sentences that build the point
- Final lines: Staccato resolution — two or three short phrases that close the thought
- Caption close: "No phone calls necessary."

Headline rules:
- No questions as hooks
- No exclamation points
- No urgency language
- One idea per post. Do not stack multiple concepts.

What Instagram does for this brand: Long-form credibility building. Clinicians and potential clients follow over time and decide Carlton is the right fit before they ever reach out. Posts should feel like they could live on the website. They are not promotional. They are educational and authoritative.

**B. LinkedIn**

LinkedIn is the primary referral-facing platform. Carlton's existing bio is the benchmark: it leads with credentials, states the population, names the methods, and notes the lived experience without over-explaining it.

Content priorities:
- Credential and licensure visibility
- Consultation and supervision availability
- Clinical perspective posts: same voice as Instagram but directed at peers
- Referral pathway clarity: make it easy for a clinician to know how to send someone

**C. Therapist Directories (Psychology Today, Alma, etc.)**

Clinicians and clients land here while actively searching. Copy needs to answer three questions in the first two sentences: What do you specialize in? Who do you see? What states are you licensed in?

Directory copy formula:
1. Sentence 1: Specialization + population
2. Sentence 2: Method + approach
3. Sentence 3: Identity affirmation signal
4. Sentence 4: State coverage + telehealth
5. Close: Barrier removal

Example:
> I specialize in ADHD, OCD, and Autism in adults, including those diagnosed later in life. My approach is insight-first. I use ERP, I-CBT, and CBT where they genuinely help, not as a default protocol. I provide affirming care for LGBTQ+ and BIPOC clients, with particular focus on men navigating identity alongside neurodivergent challenges. Fully telehealth across AZ, CA, FL, ID, IL, and WA. No phone calls necessary.

**D. Website**

Homepage: Lead with the tagline and subhead. The first sentence below them should state who this practice is for, not what Carlton does. The client's experience comes before the clinician's credentials.

About page: This is where Carlton's identity, lived experience, and the "understood sameness" positioning can be stated most directly. This is also where the men-specific focus can be named explicitly. Keep it grounded. Do not perform the identity. State it plainly and move on.

Services page: Lead each service with the client experience, not the modality. Name the method second. OCD assessment should be listed as a distinct service, not folded into general therapy.

**E. Email**

Carlton's email communication should match the website and social voice: short sentences, no filler warmth, clear next step. Avoid lengthy sign-offs. "No phone calls necessary" is appropriate in email footers and inquiry responses.

## 8. Example Rewrites

**Example 1 — The welcome message**

Before: *Welcome! I'm so glad you're here. My practice is a warm, safe space where you can explore your feelings and work toward healing.*

After: *You don't have to explain your brain from scratch. The work starts with understanding how you think, not reshaping it into something more manageable.*

Before: *I use a variety of evidence-based techniques tailored to your unique needs, including CBT, mindfulness, and more.*

**Example 3 — The call to action**

Before: *Ready to take the first step? Reach out today — I'd love to connect!*

After: *If this sounds like the right fit, scheduling is straightforward. No phone calls necessary.*

## 9. The "No Phone Calls Necessary" Device

**What It Does**

This line removes a barrier the reader did not know they were worried about. For many people, particularly neurodivergent individuals, LGBTQ+ clients, and BIPOC men, a phone call is a friction point. It requires real-time verbal performance at a moment of vulnerability. Removing it is not a convenience feature. It is a clinical access statement.

It also signals that this practice understands its clients before they arrive. A therapist who knows their clients well enough to remove the phone call barrier is a therapist who will not make you explain yourself from scratch.

**Platform: Instagram Only**

This line is an Instagram-specific closing device. Its original purpose was to signal to potential clients that booking is fully online — no office to call, no pre-screen phone call required before scheduling. On Instagram, where the next step is often unclear, the line does useful work.

On the website, the booking experience makes this point without saying it. Direct booking buttons for each path (insurance via Headway, self-pay via SimplePractice) are self-evident. The line is not needed and should not appear on the website.

**When to Use It**

- End of Instagram posts that describe a service, explain a process, or lead to a booking action
- End of directory listings and bio copy where the booking path is not visually obvious

**When Not to Use It**

- The website, in any section, including CTAs, buttons, or body copy
- Email inquiry responses (redundant; the email itself is the non-phone contact)
- Posts directed primarily at clinicians. It is client-facing language.
- Educational posts that are not leading to a call to action
- More than once per piece of content

**Rules**

- Always a standalone sentence. Never buried in a paragraph.
- Always at the end. It is a closing device, not an opener.
- No exclamation point. The deadpan delivery is the point.
- Do not vary the wording. "No phone call needed" or "No need to call" dilutes it. The exact phrase is the brand asset.
- Do not explain it. The line works because it does not justify itself.

**Functional Alternatives (use only when the exact line does not fit)**

- "Scheduling is straightforward."
- "The intake process is fully online."
- "No intake call required."

These are functional alternatives, not replacements. The original line is always preferred.
